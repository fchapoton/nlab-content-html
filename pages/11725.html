Idea

In the context of complex analytic geometry, the term “exponential exact sequence” typically referes to the short exact sequence

0→ker(exp)⟶𝔾 a⟶exp(iℏ(−))𝔾 m→0 0 \to ker(\exp) \longrightarrow \mathbb{G}_{a} \stackrel{\exp(\tfrac{i}{\hbar}(-))}{\longrightarrow} \mathbb{G}_m \to 0

given by the exponential map exp(iℏ(−))\exp(\tfrac{i}{\hbar}(-)) from the additive group to the multiplicative group. Here ℏ\hbar is any element of ℝ ×\mathbb{R}^\times (“Planck's constant”) but is traditionally set either to 11 or to 1/2π1/2 \pi.

Hence more explicitly over the complex numbers this is

0→ℏ2πℤ⟶ℂ⟶exp(iℏ(−))ℂ ×→0, 0 \to \hbar2\pi\mathbb{Z} \longrightarrow \mathbb{C} \stackrel{\exp(\tfrac{i}{\hbar}(-))}{\longrightarrow} \mathbb{C}^\times \to 0 \,,

where ℂ\mathbb{C} denotes the complex numbers as the additive abelian group and where ℂ ×=ℂ−{0}\mathbb{C}^\times = \mathbb{C} - \{0\} is the group of units of the ring structure of the complex numbers.

Often this is considered and displayed relative to a complex analytic space XX, where in terms of the structure sheaf 𝒪 X\mathcal{O}_{X} it reads

0→Lconst(ℤ)⟶𝒪 X⟶exp(iℏ(−))𝒪 X ×→0. 0 \to Lconst(\mathbb{Z}) \longrightarrow \mathcal{O}_X \stackrel{\exp(\tfrac{i}{\hbar}(-))}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{O}_X^\times \to 0 \,.

In this form the sequence is then also called the exponential sheaf sequence.

Properties

Long sequence in cohomology

The connecting homomorphisms of the long exact sequence in cohomology induces by the exponential exact sequence

H n(−,𝔾 m)⟶H n+1(−,ℤ) H^n(-,\mathbb{G}_m) \longrightarrow H^{n+1}(-,\mathbb{Z})

encode the canonical characteristic classes of line n-bundles.

  • For n=1n=1 this is the first Chern class in complex analytic geometry defined on the Picard group of holomorphic line bundles;

  • for n=2n = 2 this is the complex analytic-analog of the Dixmier-Douady class of holomorphic 2-line bundles, defined on the bigger Brauer group;

and so on.

In logarithmic geometry

In algebraic geometry there is no exponential sequence, the closest analogs being the Kummer sequence and the Artin-Schreier sequence. But in logarithmic geometry there is again a kind of exponential sequence (e.g. Ogus 01, chapter IV, remark 1.1.7, Brylinski 94, page 15). Compare also the sequences in Kato-Nakayama 99, section 1.4.
