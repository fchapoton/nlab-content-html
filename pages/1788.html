Solutions to distributional equations of the form (in generalized function-notation)

νΔ ±^(ν)=1 \nu \, \widehat{\Delta_\pm}(\nu) = 1

are given by sums of the Cauchy principal value PV(1ν)PV\left(\frac{1}{\nu}\right) with a multiple of the delta distribution (this example.):

(1)Δ ±^(ν)=PV(1ν)+cδ(ν). \widehat{\Delta_{\pm}}(\nu) \;=\; PV\left( \frac{1}{\nu}\right) + c \delta(\nu) \,.

It is clear that (1) is a tempered distribution in kk, so that our Ansatz via Fourier transform of distributions is indeed consistent so far.

We further specify this Ansatz by fixing the constant cc in (1) to c=±iπc = \pm i \pi:

Δ ±^(ν)=PV(1ν)±iπδ(ν). \widehat{\Delta_{\pm}}(\nu) \;=\; PV\left( \frac{1}{\nu}\right) \pm i\pi \delta(\nu) \,.

As before, this will be justified if we later show that the inverse Fourier transform has the required future/past cone support property.

Now by this example this particular Ansatz is in fact equal to the limit of 1/ν1/\nu with imaginary offset tending to zero (this def.)

Δ^ ±(ν)=limϵ∈(0,∞)ϵ→01ν±iϵ +. \widehat \Delta_{\pm}(\nu) \;=\; \underset{ {\epsilon \in (0,\infty)} \atop { \epsilon \to 0 } }{\lim} \frac{1}{\nu \pm i \epsilon^+} \,.

Hence in summary our Ansatz for the Fourier transformed advanced/retarded propagator is

Δ^ ±(k)=limϵ∈(0,∞)ϵ→01(k 0) 2−|k→| 2−(mcℏ) 2±iϵ +. \widehat \Delta_{\pm}(k) \;=\; \underset{ {\epsilon \in (0,\infty)} \atop { \epsilon \to 0 } }{\lim} \frac{1}{ (k_0)^2 -{\vert \vec k\vert}^2 - \left( \tfrac{m c}{\hbar} \right)^2 \pm i \epsilon^+} \,.
