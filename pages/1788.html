Here key step is the third: To obtain this in the case x>0x \gt 0 we

  1. decompose the integral over k 0k_0 as

    ∫ −∞ ∞dk 0 =+∫ −∞ 0dk 0+∫ 0 −i∞dk 0 =+∫ −i∞ 0dk 0+∫ 0 ∞dk 0; \begin{aligned} \int_{-\infty}^\infty d k_0 & = \phantom{+} \int_{-\infty}^0 d k_0 + \int_{0}^{- i \infty} d k_0 \\ & = + \int_{-i \infty}^0 d k_0 + \int_0^\infty d k_0 \,; \end{aligned}

  2. complete this to a sum of two contour integrals, the first going clockwise around the pole at −E ϵ(k→)−iϵ∈ℂ- E_\epsilon(\vec k) - i \epsilon \in \mathbb{C} in the lower half plane, the second going clockwise around +E ϵ(k→)−iϵ+ E_\epsilon(\vec k) - i \epsilon;

  3. apply Cauchy's integral formula to find the sum of the residues at these two poles (compare the corresponding computation for the causal propagator in this prop.). Observe that the minus sign in the prefactor (−2πi)(-2\pi i) comes from the fact that the contours run clockwise instead of counter-clockwise.

For x<0x \lt 0 we proceeds analogously, now completing to contours in the upper half plane. These do not enclose the two poles and hence in this case Cauchy's integral formula says that the result vanishes.

Finally, for the advanced propagator the argument is directly analogous, with two differences:

  1. now the poles sit in the upper half plane, and hence we pick up a contribution for (x−y) 0<0(x-y)^0 \lt 0 and none for (x−y) 0>0(x-y)^0 \gt 0, hence in the end a prefactor of Θ(−(x−y) 0)\Theta(-(x-y)^0) instead of Θ((x−y) 0)\Theta((x-y)^0);

  2. the contours that do contribute now go counter-clockwise, instead of counter-clockwise, and hence we pick up an extra minus sign compared to the previous computation, as befits the definition of Δ A(x,y)≔−Θ(−(x−y) 0)Δ(x,y)\Delta_A(x,y) \coloneqq -\Theta(-(x-y)^0) \Delta(x,y).
