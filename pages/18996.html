Definition

Definition

(H-cohomology)

Given a smooth manifold or formal smooth manifold XX, and given a differential 3-form H∈Ω 3(X)H \in \Omega^3(X), then forming the wedge product with HH is a nilpotent operation, and hence defines the differential of a cochain complex whose entries are the vector spaces Ω even/odd(X)\Omega^{even/odd}(X) of all odd-graded or all even-graded differential forms on XX:

⋯→Ω even(X)⟶H∧(−)Ω odd(X)⟶H∧(−)Ω even(X)⟶H∧(−)Ω odd(X)→⋯. \cdots \to \Omega^{even}(X) \overset{H\wedge(-)}{\longrightarrow} \Omega^{odd}(X) \overset{H\wedge(-)}{\longrightarrow} \Omega^{even}(X) \overset{H\wedge(-)}{\longrightarrow} \Omega^{odd}(X) \to \cdots \,.

The cochain cohomology of this cochain complex

H H∧ even/odd(X)≔;ker(Ω even/odd(X)⟶H∧(−)Ω odd/even(X))im(Ω odd/even(X)⟶H∧(−)Ω even/odd(X)) H^{even/odd}_{H\wedge}(X) \coloneqq; \frac{ ker \left( \Omega^{even/odd}(X) \overset{H\wedge(-)}{\longrightarrow} \Omega^{odd/even}(X) \right) }{ im \left( \Omega^{odd/even}(X) \overset{H\wedge(-)}{\longrightarrow} \Omega^{even/odd}(X) \right) }

is also called the H-cohomology of XX.

(Cavalcanti 03, p. 19)

Remark

Often it is assumed that the 3-form HH in def. 1 is closed, because in this case H-cohomology serves as an approximation to the corresponding HH-twisted de Rham cohomology (prop. 1 below). But of course for def. 1 to make sense in itself, HH need not be closed.

It is immediate to consider this more generally:

Definition

(H-cohomology in graded-commutative algebra)

Let 𝒜\mathcal{A} be a graded commutative algebra in characteristic zero and let H∈𝒜H \in \mathcal{A} be an odd-graded element. This implies that consecutive multiplication with HH

⋯→𝒜⟶H⋅(−)𝒜⟶H⋅(−)𝒜⟶H⋅(−)𝒜→⋯ \cdots \to \mathcal{A} \overset{H \cdot(-)}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{A} \overset{H \cdot (-)}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{A} \overset{H \cdot (-)}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{A} \to \cdots

is a chain complex.

The cochain cohomology of this cochain complex

H H⋅ even/odd(𝒜)≔;ker(𝒜 even/odd⟶H⋅(−)𝒜 odd/even)im(𝒜 odd/even⟶H⋅(−)𝒜 even/odd) H^{even/odd}_{H\cdot}(\mathcal{A}) \coloneqq; \frac{ ker \left( \mathcal{A}_{even/odd} \overset{H \cdot(-)}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{A}_{odd/even} \right) }{ im \left( \mathcal{A}_{odd/even} \overset{H\cdot(-)}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{A}_{even/odd} \right) }

is the H-cohomology of 𝒜\mathcal{A}.

Properties

Proposition

If the de Rham complex (Ω •(X),d dr)(\Omega^\bullet(X),d_{dr}) is formal, then for H∈Ω cl 3(X)H \in \Omega_{cl}^3(X) a closed differential 3-form, the H-cohomology of XX (def. 1) coincides with its HH-twisted de Rham cohomology:

H H∧(−) •(X)≃H d+H∧(−) •(X). H^\bullet_{H \wedge (-)}(X) \simeq H^\bullet_{d + H \wedge (-)}(X) \,.

(Cavalcanti 03, theorem 1.6).

Proposition

(H-cohomology of sum of decomposable forms is non-vanishing)

If HH is a finite sum of decomposables

H=∑k=1nα k⏟deg=1∧β k⏟deg=2 H \;=\; \underoverset{k = 1}{n}{\sum} \underset{deg = 1}{\underbrace{\alpha_k}} \wedge \underset{deg = 2}{\underbrace{\beta_k}}

then its H-cohomology (def. 2) does not vanish.

(Sackel 18)

Examples

Odd symplectic forms

Example

(H-cohomology of odd symplectic form)

Let XX be a finite dimensional graded manifold in degrees 0 and 1 and equipped with a symplectic form ω\omega of odd coordinate degree (e.g. the symplectic form on a Poisson Lie algebroid when regarded as a symplectic Lie n-algebroid).

Then every H-cohomology-class (def. 2) for H=ωH = \omega has a unique representative which on any local coordinate chart of Darboux coordinates (x i,p i) i∈{1,⋯,n}(x^i, p_i)_{i \in \{1, \cdots, n\}} is of the form

f(x,p)dx 1∧⋯∧dx n. f(x,p) d x^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge d x^n \,.

(Ševera 05, p. 1)

The decomposed supergravity C-field

under construction

We try to discuss the H-cohomology (def. 2) of the canonical degree-3 element (defined in (3) below) in the DF-algebra (recalled in (1) below), which serves as a super-exceptional geometry-tangent space-wise model of the supergravity C-field.

The DF-Algebra

Let

(1) CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32) ≔Sym((e a⏟deg=(1,even)) a∈{0,1,⋯,10},(B ab=−B ba⏟deg=(1,even)) a,b∈{0,1,⋯,10},(ψ α⏟deg=(1,odd)) α∈{1,⋯,32},(η α⏟deg=(1,odd)) α∈{1,⋯,32}) \begin{aligned} & CE\left( T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}} \right) \\ & \coloneqq\; Sym\left( ( \underset{ deg = (1,even) }{ \underbrace{ e^{a} }} )_{a \in \{0,1,\cdots, 10\}} \,, ( \underset{ deg = (1,even) }{ \underbrace{ B^{a b} = - B^{b a} }} )_{a,b \in \{0,1,\cdots, 10\}} \,, ( \underset{ deg = (1,odd) }{ \underbrace{ \psi^\alpha }} )_{\alpha \in \{1,\cdots, 32\}} \,, ( \underset{ deg = (1,odd) }{ \underbrace{ \eta^\alpha }} )_{\alpha \in \{1,\cdots, 32\}} \right) \end{aligned}

be the ℤ×(ℤ/2)\mathbb{Z} \times (\mathbb{Z}/2)-bi-graded-commutative algebra which is freely generated over the field of real numbers as shown.

Here for α 1\alpha_1, α 2∈CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32)\alpha_2 \in CE\left(T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}}\right) two elements of homogeneous bi-degree (n 1,σ 1),(n 2,σ 2)∈ℤ×(ℤ/2)(n_1, \sigma_1), (n_2, \sigma_2) \in \mathbb{Z} \times (\mathbb{Z}/2), respectively, the sign rule is

α 1∧α 2=(−1) n 1n 2+σ 1σ 2α 2∧α 1. \alpha_1 \wedge \alpha_2 \;=\; (-1)^{n_1 n_2 + \sigma_1 \sigma_2} \, \alpha_2 \wedge \alpha_1 \,.

(See at signs in supergeometry the section the sign rule from internalization.)

This is the graded-commutative algebra that underlies the DF-algebra at parameter s=−6s = -6, which in turn is the Chevalley-Eilenberg algebra of a super Lie algebra that is a fermionic extension of the “M-theory super Lie algebra” (Bandos-Azcarraga-Izquierdo-Picon-Varela 04, section 3, based on D’Auria-Fré 82, section 6).

There is an action of the Spin group Spin(10,1)Spin(10,1) on the algebra CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32)CE\left( T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}} \right) (1) which

  1. on the linear span of the (e a)(e^a) is the defining representation of the proper orthochronous Lorentz group (i.e. the vector representation),

  2. on the linear span of the (B ab=−B ba)(B^{ab} = - B^{b a}) the corresponding bivector representation;

  3. on the linear span of the (ψ α)(\psi^\alpha) as well as on that of the (η α)(\eta^\alpha) is the real spin representation 32\mathbf{32} (the Majorana-Weyl spinor representation of Spin(10,1)Spin(10,1)).

We write

(2)CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32) Spin(10,1)↪CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32) CE(T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}})^{Spin(10,1)} \hookrightarrow CE(T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}})

for the invariant part of the algebra under this spin group-action.

In the following we use the Einstein summation convention with respect to the Minkowski metric on ℝ 10,1\mathbb{R}^{10,1}. For example

e a∧B a b≔−e 0B 0b+∑a∈{1,⋯,10}e a∧B ab e^a \wedge B_a{}^b \;\coloneqq\; - e^0 B^{0 b} + \underset{a \in \{1, \cdots, 10\}}{\sum} e^a \wedge B^{a b}

for vector indices, and

ψ¯Γ aη ≔ψ¯ α(Γ a) α βη β ≔∑α,β∈{1,⋯,32}ψ¯ α(Γ a) α βη β \begin{aligned} \overline{\psi} \Gamma_a \eta & \coloneqq \overline{\psi}_\alpha (\Gamma_a)^\alpha{}_\beta \eta^\beta \\ & \coloneqq \underset{ \alpha,\beta \in \{1, \cdots, 32\} }{\sum} \overline{\psi}_\alpha (\Gamma_a)^\alpha{}_\beta \eta^\beta \end{aligned}

for spinor indices, where (Γ a)(\Gamma_a) is a representation of the Clifford algebra and where −¯\overline{-} denotes the Majorana conjugate or equivalently (by this prop.) the Dirac conjugate.

In terms of this notation, the Spin(10,1)Spin(10,1)-invariants are precisely those elements “all whose indices are contracted via the Einstein summation convention”. For example

ψ¯Γ abη∧B ab∈CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32) Spin(10,1). \overline{\psi} \Gamma_{a b} \eta \wedge B^{a b} \;\in\; CE\left( T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}}\right)^{Spin(10,1)} \,.

There is a differential on (1), making it a differential graded-commutative algebra, that extends the Chevalley-Eilenberg algebra of the super Minkowski spacetime ℝ 10,1|32\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}} and such that the image of the 3-form HH below in (3) under this differential is the the M2-brane higher WZW term μ M2≔i2ψ¯Γ abψ∧e a∧e b\mu_{M2} \coloneqq \tfrac{i}{2}\overline{\psi} \Gamma_{a b} \psi \wedge e^a \wedge e^b). This is what makes the DF-algebra interesting in itself. But since this differential plays no role for the discussion of just H-cohomology (def. 2), it is of no concern here.

The decomposed C-field

In the DF-algebra (1), consider the following element of bi-degree (3,even)(3,even)

(3)H ≔−13⋅5!B a b∧B c c∧B c a⏟H fib =+14!B ab∧e a∧e b⏟H base,bos+i4⋅5!η¯(10Γ ae a+iΓ abB ab)ψ⏟H base,ferm. \begin{aligned} H & \coloneqq\; \underset{ H_{fib} }{ \underbrace{ - \tfrac{1}{3 \cdot 5!} B^a{}_b \wedge B^c{}_c \wedge B^c{}_a }} \\ & \phantom{=} + \underset{ H_{base,bos} }{ \underbrace{ \tfrac{1}{4!} B_{a b} \wedge e^a \wedge e^b }} + \underset{ H_{base,ferm} }{ \underbrace{ \tfrac{i}{4 \cdot 5!} \overline{\eta} \left( 10 \Gamma_a e^a + i \Gamma_{a b} B^{a b} \right) \psi }} \end{aligned} \,.

(Bandos-Azcarraga-Izquierdo-Picon-Varela 04, (37))

We want to compute the H-cohomology (def. 2) of this element.

Strategy: Compute the H-cohomology of each summand in (3) separately with the technique from (Ševera 05, p. 1), then use the spectral sequence of a double complex, twice, to put the results together.

Number operators

For analyzing the HH-cohomology of (3) it will be useful to consider various countings of numbers of generators.

First of all there is the plain number of generators:

Write

n e,n b:CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32)⟶CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32) n_e, n_b \;\colon\; CE\left( T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}} \right) \longrightarrow CE\left( T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}} \right)

for the linear maps which on monomials are given by multiplication with the number of generators e ae^a or the number of generators B abB^{a b}, respectively.

These are equivalently given by

n e=e a∧∂ e a n_e \;=\; e^a \wedge \partial_{e^a}

and

(4)n B=12B a b∧∂ B a b n_B \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} B^{a}{}_b \wedge \partial_{B^{a}{}_b}

Then there are mixed number operator, which are more subtle. We write:

(5)n B,B≔−14B a b∧B b a∧∂ B a b∂ B b a. n_{B,B} \;\coloneqq\; - \frac{1}{4} B^{a}{}_b \wedge B^b{}_a \wedge \partial_{B^a{}_b} \partial_{B^b{}_a} \,.

In order to understand these, consider the further subalgebra

(6)CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32) Bsimp Spin(10,1)↪CE(T exc,−6ℝ 10,1|32) Spin(10,1) CE\left( T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}} \right)^{Spin(10,1)}_{B simp} \hookrightarrow CE\left( T_{exc,-6}\mathbb{R}^{10,1\vert\mathbf{32}} \right)^{Spin(10,1)}

of the Spin(10,1)Spin(10,1)-invariant part of the DF-algebra (2) of these elements which do not contain contractions B a b∧B b cB^{a}{}_{b}\wedge B^b{}_c of BB-s with themselves.

Claim. The mixed number operator n B,Bn_{B,B} (5) vanishes on the subalgebra (6).

Proof: Observe that n B,Bn_{B,B} commutes with the Spin(10,1)Spin(10,1)-action (since its algebraic expression has all indices contracted correctly). But if n B,Bn_{B,B} hits an invariant term with more than one BB-generator in it, but not contracted among themselves, then the result breaks the Einstein summation-pattern of the indices, and hence is not Spin(10,1)Spin(10,1)-invariant anymore, unless it vanishes.

The fiber term

We discuss the H-cohomology (def. 2) of the fiber summand in (3)

H fib≔B a b∧B b c∧B c a. H_{fib} \;\coloneqq\; B^a{}_b \wedge B^b{}_c \wedge B^c{}_a \,.

As a candidate homotopy operator, consider

The base term

We discuss the H-cohomology (def. 2) of the bosonic base summand in (3)

H base,bos≔B ab∧e a∧e b H_{base,bos} \;\coloneqq\; B_{a b} \wedge e^a \wedge e^b
