Idea

A character of a group GG is a group homomorphism into the circle group U(1)U(1), or more generally into the group of units k ×k^\times of a given ground field (for instance ℂ ×=U(1)\mathbb{C}^\times = U(1)):

χ:G⟶k ×. \chi \;\colon\; G \longrightarrow k^\times \,.

Since k ×k^\times is an abelian group, this means that group characters are in particular class functions.

Dually a co-character is a homomorphism out of k ×k^\times into GG.

The collection of characters is itself an abelian group under the pointwise multiplication, this is called the character lattice Hom(G,k ×) Hom(G,k^\times) of the group. Similarly the cocharacter lattice is Hom(k ×,G)Hom(k^\times, G).

For topological groups one considers continuous characters. Specifically, for a locally compact Hausdorff group GG (often further assumed to be an abelian group), a character of GG is continuous homomorphism to the circle group ℝ/ℤ\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}. If GG is profinite, then this is the same as an continuous homomorphism to the discrete group ℚ/ℤ\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}. (See MO.)

Properties

Restriction of group characters to maximal tori – weights

Let GG be a connected compact Lie group.

By the general properties of maximal tori in this case, it follows that every group character G→U(1)G \to U(1) is already fixed by its restriction along a maximal torus inclusion

T↪G→U(1). T \hookrightarrow G \to U(1) \,.

Now the group characters of the abelian maximal torus T≃U(1) nT\simeq U(1)^n are n-tuples of group characters of the circle group U(1)U(1), which are integers – the weights.

Explicitly, under a given identification of the circle group as a quotient of the additive group of real numbers

U(1)≃ℝ/hℤ U(1) \simeq \mathbb{R}/h \mathbb{Z}

for h∈(0,∞)h \in (0,\infty), then the character λ\lambda on U(1) n≃TU(1)^n\simeq T labeled by (x 1,⋯,x n)∈ℤ n(x_1, \cdots, x_n) \in \mathbb{Z}^n is

λ(t 1,⋯t n)=exp(iℏ∑ j=1 nt jn j) \lambda(t_1,\cdots t_n) = \exp(\tfrac{i}{\hbar} \sum_{j = 1}^n t_j n_j )

(where ℏ≔h/2π\hbar \coloneqq h/2\pi is “Planck's constant”).

(e.g. Johansen, section 2.10)

Relation to Chern roots and the splitting principle

A group character, hence a group homomorphism G→U(1)G \to U(1) induces a map of classifying spaces BG→BU(1)≃K(ℤ,2)B G \to B U(1) \simeq K(\mathbb{Z},2). Similarly for the restriction to the maximal torus above, which induces

BU(1) n≃BT→BG→BU(1)≃K(ℤ,2). B U(1)^n \simeq B T \to B G \to B U(1)\simeq K(\mathbb{Z},2) \,.

Under this identification the weights x ix_i of the group character, as above, are the “Chern roots” as the appear in the splitting principle. See there for more.

Characters and fundamental group of tori

Write S 1S^1 for the circle group.

Let TT be a torus, regarded as an abelian group. Write [T,S 1][T,S^1] for its character group.

There is a bilinear form

π 1(T)⊗[T,S 1]⟶ℤ \pi_1(T)\otimes [T, S^1] \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z}

on the fundamental group of the torus and its character group, given by sending a homotopy class [γ][\gamma] of a continuous map

γ:S 1⟶T \gamma \colon S^1 \longrightarrow T

to the homotopy class c(γ)c(\gamma) of the composition with a character c:T⟶S 1c \colon T \longrightarrow S^1

c(γ):S 1⟶γT⟶cS 1 c(\gamma) \;\colon\; S^1 \stackrel{\gamma}{\longrightarrow} T \stackrel{c}{\longrightarrow} S^1

regarded as an element [c(γ)]∈π 1(S 1)≃ℤ[c(\gamma)] \in \pi_1(S^1) \simeq \mathbb{Z}.

This bilinear form is non-degenerate, and hence constitutes an isomorphism

π 1(T)≃[T,S 1]. \pi_1(T) \simeq [T,S^1] \,.

Inner product and orthogonality

The complex class functions on a finite group GG have an inner product given by

⟨α,β⟩≔1|G|∑g∈Gα(g)β(g)¯. \langle \alpha, \beta\rangle \coloneqq \frac{1}{{\vert G \vert}} \underset{g \in G}{\sum} \alpha(g) \overline{\beta(g)} \,.

The Schur orthogonality relation is the statement that the irreducible group characters {χ i} i\{\chi_i\}_i form an orthonormal basis of the space of class functions under this inner product:

⟨χ i,χ j⟩={1 ifi=j 0 otherwise \langle \chi_i, \chi_j \rangle = \left\{ \array{ 1 & if \; i = j \\ 0 & otherwise } \right.

Such properties arise from characters occurring as traces of group representations.

In terms of the classifying space of the group

Consider the classifying space, BGB G, of the group. Then its free loop space, Map(S 1,BG)Map (S^1, B G), has as components GG modulo conjugation. Then, the characters of GG may be expressed as the zeroth cohomology of this loop space, H 0(ℒBG,ℂ)H^0(\mathcal{L} B G, \mathbb{C}). This construction is useful in the generalisation to transchromatic characters.
