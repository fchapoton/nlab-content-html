Idea

For any nn, the category StrnCatStr n Cat of strict n-categories is cartesian closed. If CC and DD are strict nn-categories, the objects of the exponential object D CD^C are strict nn-functors C→DC\to D, its morphisms are strict nn-natural transformations, and likewise its higher cells are strict higher transfors.

On the other hand, one expects the weak (n+1)(n+1)-category nCatn Cat of weak n-categories to be cartesian closed in the (n+1)(n+1)-categorical sense, so that one has equivalences of hom-nn-categories

(1)nCat(C×D,E)≃nCat(C,Hom(D,E)).n Cat(C\times D, E) \simeq n Cat(C, Hom(D,E)).

In this case, Hom(D,E)Hom(D,E) should consist of weak functors, pseudo transformations (which are natural up to coherent equivalence), and pseudo higher transfors. Note that although C×DC\times D (probably) means the same thing in the strict and weak worlds, D CD^C and Hom(D,E)Hom(D,E) are not the same, and in general not even equivalent.

Gray tensor products are part of the general philosophy of trying to understand weak higher structures without getting bogged down in the theory of weaker higher-er structures. One instance of this philosophy is the use of model categories (or more general types of homotopy theory) to use strict functors to model weak ones. Although not every weak functor is equivalent to a strict one (already when n=2n=2), one expects this to become true if the domain is suitably cofibrant. This is a theorem for small values of nn and can be regarded as a definition of “weak functor” for large values of nn; see the folk model structure for 2-categories and this paper.

Gray tensor products attempt to extend this to deal with weak kk-transfors for k>0k\gt 0, starting with pseudo (or lax or oplax) natural transformations, by equipping higher categories of some sort with a non-cartesian monoidal and/or closed structure. This began with John Gray, who showed that the category Str2CatStr2Cat is a non-symmetric closed monoidal category in which one internal-hom Lax(C,D)Lax(C,D) consists of strict 2-functors, lax natural transformations, and modifications (the other contains oplax transformations instead). A straightforward modification produces a closed symmetric monoidal structure based on pseudo natural transformations; the monoidal product is now called the Gray tensor product.

Later various authors constructed analogous tensor products for strict ω-categories and strict ω-groupoids, here called the Crans-Gray tensor product. Here the internal-homs consists of strict ω\omega-functors and lax/oplax kk-transfors for k>0k\gt 0. (It appears to be unknown whether there is a pseudo version.)

The Problem

The (pseudo version of the) Gray tensor product of 2-categories is used to define Gray-categories, which are a version of semistrict 3-categories. This is very convenient because Gray-categories are just categories enriched over (Str2Cat,⊗ Gray)(Str2Cat,\otimes_{Gray}), so that all of enriched category theory applies to them (as it does to 2-categories, being categories enriched over CatCat). Thus, it is natural to hope that one might be able to iterate this process, defining a “Gray tensor product” of semistrict nn-categories and then defining semistrict (n+1)(n+1)-categories to be categories enriched over semistrict nn-categories.

However, there is a problem:

  • No category SSnCatSS n Cat of algebraic nn-categories with weak interchange and strict functors can admit a biclosed structure that contains non-strict transformations.

Now there are lots of adjectives in this statement; for now just notice that the progression “categories, 2-categories, Gray-categories, Gray-cat-categories?” is producing categories of algebraic nn-categories with weak interchange and strict functors. So if this no-go statement is true, then Gray-cat does not admit a biclosed structure of the desired sort, and Gray-cat-categories probably can’t be defined correctly.

To see why this no-go statement holds, imagine some hypothetical algebraic notion of semistrict nn-category with weak interchange, and consider what sort of transformation (that is, 1-transfor) we want to model. Let CC and DD be semistrict nn-categories and F,G:C→DF,G:C\to D (strict) functors; then a transformation α:F→G\alpha:F\to G should probably consist of

  • components α x:F(x)→G(x)\alpha_x: F(x) \to G(x),
  • equivalences α f:G(f)∘α x≃α yF(f)\alpha_f: G(f) \circ \alpha_x \simeq \alpha_y F(f) for f:x→yf:x\to y,
  • and higher data,
  • satisfying axioms.

Now the question is, how is α gf\alpha_{g f} related to the composite of α g\alpha_g and α f\alpha_f? For pseudonatural transformations between 2-categories, they are equal (both being 2-cells, there is no room for anything weaker). For a “fully weak” notion of transformation in higher dimensions, one might expect them to be only equivalent—but for the sorts of transfors used in the internal-hom for the Crans-Gray tensor product of ω\omega-categories, it turns out that they are also equal. In fact, if the transformations are to give us a biclosed category of nn-categories and strict functors, it turns out that they must be equal.

Consider the following: a transformation F⇒G:C→DF\Rightarrow G: C\to D should be the same as a functor 2→[C,D]2 \to [C,D] where 22 is the interval nn-category. But if we are to have a closed category SSnCatSS n Cat, giving such a functor should be equivalent to giving a functor C→[2,D]C\to [2,D]. (This doesn’t depend on symmetry, but if things are not symmetric then [−,−][-,-] means something different in the two cases.) Now inspection of how this would work reveals that the relationship of α gf\alpha_{g f} to α g\alpha_g and α f\alpha_f in the first picture corresponds to the functoriality of C→[2,D]C\to [2,D] in the second picture. That is, if α gf=α g.α f\alpha_{g f} = \alpha_g . \alpha_f (and so on) then the functor C→[2,D]C\to [2,D] is a strict functor, while if it is only equivalent, then C→[2,D]C\to [2,D] will be only a weak functor. Hence, if we allow only strict functors, then all our transformations must be “semistrict” in the sense that α gf=α g.α f\alpha_{g f} = \alpha_g . \alpha_f.

Now, however, we meet the second horn of the dilemma: in an nn-category with weak interchange, semistrict transformations need not be composable! Suppose that α:F→G\alpha: F \to G and β:G→H\beta: G\to H are semistrict transformations. Then we can define the components of a transformation β.α:F→H\beta.\alpha:F\to H in the obvious way, but is it semistrict? If you try to prove that it is, you’ll find that you need a strict interchange law. (There is a pentagon involving all the ways to compose a 2x2 grid of square 2-cells, a picture of which can be found in Crans’ paper cited below.) So we are stuck.

What can we do? Well, for one thing, the theorem is only about closed structures, not about monoidal structures. And Sjoerd Crans did manage to construct a non-closed “Gray-like” monoidal structure on Gray-cat. But it’s not clear whether the resulting notion of “Gray-cat-category” is in fact semistrict (i.e. whether arbitrary 4-categories could be strictified to them).

Another way to escape would be to allow weak functors. This is not much good all by itself, since algebraic nn-categories and weak functors do not generally form very good categories. They don’t have many limits and colimits, for one thing, and the theory of enriched categories isn’t much good when the enriching category doesn’t have limits and colimits. However, if we instead use a geometric definition of higher category, then we can get well-behaved 1-categories containing weak functors. And it is known for many nonalgebraic notions of higher category that there are good Gray-like tensor products; in fact, in many cases it is actually the cartesian product (an important exception being the lax version of the Verity-Gray tensor product of weak complicial sets). This is one reason why non-algebraic notions of higher category are very attractive.

Finally, a third escape that suggests itself would be to look for a notion of algebraic semistrict nn-categories which nevertheless has strict interchange. As far as I know, the only work on strictly interchanging semistrict categories is one nonalgebraic notion which depends on weak units. The prospects for algebraic notions don’t seem that appealing, however, since the whole idea of the Gray-approach was to build in the semistrictness via the iterated enrichment, but while it is easy to imagine weakening interchange this way, it is hard to see how this could produce strict interchange but weak units.
