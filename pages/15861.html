Idea

In a co-Heyting algebra it is possible to define an equivalent to the boundary operation in topology. In applications this affords an intrinsic view of mereology without consideration of the embedding of bodies into an ambient space.

Definition

Let aa be an element of a co-Heyting algebra LL with subtraction \\backslash and co-Heyting negation ∼\sim. The (co-Heyting) boundary of aa is defined as ∂a:=a∧∼a\partial a :=a\wedge\sim a.

Properties

  • ∂(a∧b)=(∂a∧b)∨(a∧∂b)\partial (a\wedge b) = (\partial a \wedge b)\vee (a\wedge\partial b)\quad. (Leibniz rule)

  • ∂(a∨b)∨∂(a∧b)=∂a∨∂b\partial (a\vee b)\vee\partial (a\wedge b) =\partial a\vee\partial b\quad.

  • The boundaries x=∂ax=\partial a can be characterized as those xx with ∂x=x\partial x = x (in particular, ∂ 2=∂\partial^2=\partial), or, alternatively, those xx with ∼x=1\sim x=1, showing that boundary parts are precisely the (intuitively) thin parts.

  • Every part is the sum of its regular core and its boundary: a=∼∼a∨∂aa=\sim\sim a\vee\partial a. This suggests to view ∂a\partial a as the irregular part of aa.

Remark

As the lattice of subtoposes of a given topos ℰ\mathcal{E} comes naturally with a co-Heyting structure it becomes (in principle) possible to define the boundary ∂𝒜\partial\mathcal{A} of a subtopos 𝒜\mathcal{A} in this lattice and then in turn the boundary ∂T′\partial T' of the geometric theory T′T' that 𝒜\mathcal{A} classifies which is an extension of the theory TT classified by ℰ\mathcal{E} (Lawvere 1991, Caramello 2009).1

When a subtopos 𝒜\mathcal{A} is complemented in the lattice of subtoposes, as occurs e.g. for open, closed or locally closed subtoposes, its boundary vanishes: ∂𝒜=0\partial\mathcal{A}=0 since in a co-Heyting algebra the complement ¬a\neg a necessarily coincides with ∼a\sim a (cf. at co-Heyting negation).

References

Closed boundaries of subtoposes are defined in an exercise of SGA4 (cf. at open subtopos for some of the details). The concept for co-Heyting algebras seems to stem from Lawvere (1976,1986, 1991) although the 1927 article of M. Zarycki already studies properties and axiomatic potential of the boundary operator in topology. La Palme Reyes, Reyes&Zolfaghari (2004) has an introductory exposition in the context of bi-Heyting algebras. For boundaries of geometric theories Caramello (2009) is essential reading although they don’t appear there explicitly. For mereological applications of the concept see Lawvere (1986), Stell&Worboys (1997), Pagliani (2009) and Mormann (2013).
