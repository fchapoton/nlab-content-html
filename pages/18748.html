Idea

In flat geometry the parallel vectors with the same orientation form an equivalence class. If we neglect the orientation we also get (a bigger) equivalence class. The same holds for oriented lines and even higher dimensional oriented subspaces. An equivalence class, usually with the orientation taken into account, is called a direction (in the case of higher dimension, nn-direction, see also bivector). It can be attached to a vector, oriented line etc. and in the normed case characterized by a unit vector of the same direction.

Definition

For VV a real vector space, the direction of a non-zero vector v∈Vv \in V is its equivalence class under the multiplication with positive real numbers.

Recall that a nonzero vector is parallel to a subspace WW of a vector space VV if it has a representative of the form AB→\vec{A B} with A,B∈WA,B\in W. Two ponzero vectors are mutually parallel if one is a nonzero multiple of another.

Direction of a nonzero vector in a vector space over any field is geometrically defined as the equivalence class of lines parallel to the vector. Sometimes one talks oriented lines instead (In complex case one gives “real orientation” on complex lines by choosing a real subspace of real dimension 11.)

If VV is a normed vector space or inner product spaces in particular (as they have the induced norm v↦⟨v,v⟩v\mapsto \sqrt{\langle v, v\rangle}), the (oriented) direction of a nonzero vector vv is represented by the unit direction vector (with the same direction in any of the above senses) and it can be expressed by the formula

v/|v|∈S(V). v/{\vert v\vert} \;\in \; S(V) \,.

and is an element of the unit sphere S(V)S(V) of VV. (e.g MathWorld).

Similarly the unoriented direction of a non-zero vector in any vector space is its equivalence class under multiplication with any non-zero element of the ground field, hence the element it represents in the corresponding projective space PVP V.

The unit direction vector can be atached not only to a (direction of) a vector but also to a direction of lines per se (see Wikipedia, Direction vector).

J. Lelong-Ferrand defines the direction of an affine subspace of an affine space as its orbit under the action of the translation group. This agrees with the parallelness in Choquet where two affine subspaces of the same dimension are parallel if their translation group of vectors (as a subgroup of the group of vectors of translations of the entire space) is the same.
