Idea

An overt space is a space that satisfies a condition logically dual to that satisfied by a compact space. An overt space is also called open (in French, there is only one word, ‘ouvert’).

Motivation

Notice (see here) that a topological space XX is compact if and only if, for every other space YY and any open subspace UU of X×YX \times Y, the subspace

∀ XU={b:Y|∀a:X,(a,b)∈U} \forall_X U = \{ b\colon Y \;|\; \forall\; a\colon X,\; (a, b) \in U \}

is open in YY.

Dually, a space is overt if and only if, for every other space YY and any open subspace UU of X×YX \times Y, the subspace

∃ XU={b:Y|∃a:X,(a,b)∈U} \exists_X U = \{ b\colon Y \;|\; \exists\; a\colon X,\; (a, b) \in U \}

is open in YY. (Note that the duality here is only in the logical connectives, not within the category of spaces.)

Of course, every topological space satisfies this condition; ∃ XU\exists_X U is the union over aa of the open subspaces {b|(a,b)∈U}\{ b \;|\; (a,b) \in U \}. However, the condition is not trivial in some frameworks, such as constructive locale theory, formal topology, and Abstract Stone Duality.

Definition

To remove it from dependence on points, we write the definition like this:

A space (topological space, locale, etc) XX is overt (or open) if, given any space YY and any open subspace UU of the product space X×YX \times Y, there exists an open ∃ XU\exists_X U in YY that satisfies the universal property of existential quantification:

∃ XU⊆V⇔U⊆X×V \exists_X U \subseteq V \;\Leftrightarrow\; U \subseteq X \times V

for every open VV in YY.

Note that, since we quantify over all spaces YY and opens U⊆X×YU\subseteq X\times Y in this definition, whether a given space is overt may depend on precisely what ‘space’ and ‘open’ mean (even if XX is an example for both meanings). For example, if XX is a set with the discrete topology, then it is always overt if ‘space’ means ‘topological space’ or ‘locale’ with the usual meaning of ‘open’. On the other hand, if ‘space’ means simply ‘set’, but ‘open’ refers to the synthetic notion induced by a dominance, then overtness of XX is a nontrivial condition (and in fact, if all sets are overt in this sense then the dominance is trivial).

Overt locales

In the case of locales, it is sufficient to require the above definition only for the case Y≔1Y \coloneqq 1 the point (unlike the “dual” case of compactness). In this case, when the map ∃ X:Op(X)→Op(1)=TV\exists_X\colon Op(X) \to Op(1) = TV (the set or class of truth values) exists, it is a positivity predicate on Op(X)Op(X). The behavior of this predicate depends on the foundational assumptions:

  • In classical mathematics, it always exists; thus classically every locale is overt.

  • In impredicative constructive mathematics (such as the internal logic of a topos), the positivity predicate can be defined, but it may not satisfy the requisite univeral property of adjointness. Thus, constructively, not every locale is overt. However, even constructively, every topological locale is overt (so a sober space is overt regardless of whether it is viewed as a topological space or as a locale).

  • In constructive predicative mathematics, a positivity predicate cannot be defined and so must be given as a structure on (predicative data that generate) the locale, as is done with a formal topology. Once this structure is assumed, one can then ask whether a formal topology is overt, i.e. whether the axiomatic positivity predicate satisfies the requisite adjointness.

In impredicative constructive mathematics, overt locales can be characterized by the positive covering lemma: XX is overt iff every open U∈O(X)U\in O(X) can be covered by positive opens, and iff every covering of an open UU can be refined to a covering by positive elements.

Examples

  • At least in impredicative constructive mathematics, the locale induced by a topological space is overt. An open subspace UU is positive if and only if it is inhabited.

  • At least in impredicative constructive mathematics, the spectrum of a commutative ring AA (defined as the locale whose frame is the frame of radical ideals) is overt if and only if any element of AA is nilpotent or not nilpotent.

Remarks

As compact spaces go with proper maps, so overt spaces go with open maps. Indeed, XX is compact if and only if the unique map X→ptX \to pt to the point is proper, while XX is overt if and only if the unique map X→ptX \to pt is open. Similarly, if X→ptX\to pt is instead closed, then XX is covert.

Note that overtness is expressed only in terms of a left adjoint, whereas open maps of locales must additionally satisfy a Frobenius reciprocity condition. In the case of locale maps to the point, this latter condition is automatic.

An overt space may also be called locally (−1)(-1)-connected, since this condition is the (0,1)-topos-theoretic version of the notion of locally connected topos and locally n-connected (n,1)-topos. A similar thing happens for higher local connectivity involving the Frobenius reciprocity condition, which must be imposed on general geometric morphisms to make them locally nn-connected, but is automatic for morphisms to the point.

In synthetic topology

In synthetic topology, we interpret ‘space’ to mean simply ‘set’ (or type, i.e. the basic objects of our foundational system). If the notion of ‘open’ is specified by a dominance, then there is an induced nontrivial notion of “overt set”, defined essentially as above. For instance, the Rosolini dominance is the smallest dominance such that ℕ\mathbb{N} is overt, whereas if all sets are overt then the dominance is trivial.

On the other hand, if by ‘open’ we mean an open subset in the sense of Penon, then all sets are overt.

References
