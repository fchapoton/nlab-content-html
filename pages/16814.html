Idea

While a (left or right) adjoint to a functor may be understood as the best approximation (from one side or the other) of a possibly non-existent inverse, any pair of adjoint functors restricts to an equivalence of categories on subcategories. These subcategories are sometimes known as the center of the adjunction, their objects are sometimes known as the fixed points of the adjunction.

The equivalences of categories that arise from fixed points of adjunctions this way are often known as dualities. Examples include Pontrjagin duality, Gelfand duality, Stone duality, and the Isbell duality between commutative rings and affine schemes.

Definition

If F:𝒟⟶𝒞F \colon \mathcal{D} \longrightarrow \mathcal{C} is left adjoint to G:𝒞⟶𝒟G \colon \mathcal{C} \longrightarrow \mathcal{D}, then we take the fixed points of the endofunctor FGF G to be those objects of 𝒞\mathcal{C} on which the counit ϵ\epsilon is an isomorphism, and take the fixed points of GFG F to be those objects of 𝒟\mathcal{D} on which the unit η\eta is an isomorphism. The triangle identities then imply that FF and GG induce an equivalence of categories between the full subcategories of fixed points of FGF G and the fixed points of GFG F.

Properties

  • If the adjunction is idempotent, then the fixed objects in 𝒞\mathcal{C} are precisely those of the form GdG d, and dually the fixed objects in 𝒟\mathcal{D} are those of the form FcF c. Indeed, this is essentially the definition of an idempotent adjunction.

Examples

Gelfand duality

Gelfand duality is the fixed point equivalence of an adjunction between compactly generated Hausdorff spaces and topological algebras over the complex numbers, given by using the complex numbers as dualizing object (Porst-Tholen 91, section 4-c).
