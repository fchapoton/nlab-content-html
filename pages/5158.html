Definitions

There are several things that one might mean by a “(possibly weak) homotopy equivalence of toposes (or (∞,1)-toposes).” Some candidates are:

  1. A geometric morphism that induces an equivalence of the fundamental ∞-groupoid of a locally ∞-connected (∞,1)-topos, or more generally of shapes.

  2. A geometric morphism which induces an isomorphism on all nonabelian cohomology with coefficients in constant ∞-stacks.

  3. A geometric morphism which induces an isomorphism on all abelian sheaf cohomology with coefficients in locally constant sheaves (of complexes) of abelian groups, as well as nonabelian cohomology in dimension one (the most classical notion).

  4. A geometric morphism which has an inverse up to “homotopy” in the classical sense of a map E×[0,1]→FE\times [0,1]\to F, where since EE and FF are toposes, we have to incarnate [0,1][0,1] as the topos Sh([0,1])Sh([0,1]).

Of these, the first three are arguably a notion of weak homotopy equivalence.

Relationship between definitions.

The first two definitions are equivalent, since (viewing a topos EE equivalently as the (∞,1)(\infty,1)-topos of sheaves on the same site, if necessary), a constant ∞-stack on EE is one of the form LConstALConst A for an ∞-groupoid AA, and the cohomology of EE with coefficients in LConstALConst A is just π 0(Hom E(*,LConstA))\pi_0(Hom_E(*,LConst A)). But when EE is locally ∞\infty-connected, Π E\Pi_E is left adjoint to LConstLConst, so this is the same as π 0Hom ∞Gpd(Π E(*),A)=Hom Ho(∞Gpd)(Π ∞(E),A)\pi_0 Hom_{\infty Gpd}(\Pi_E(*),A) = Hom_{Ho(\infty Gpd)}(\Pi_\infty(E),A) by definition of Π ∞(E)\Pi_\infty(E). Thus, by the Yoneda lemma, a map of toposes induces an equivalence of fundamental ∞-groupoids, i.e. an isomorphism in Ho(∞Gpd)Ho(\infty Gpd), iff it induces an isomorphism on all such cohomology. Since in the non–locally-∞\infty-connected case, the shape is just the functor that would be represented by Π ∞(E)\Pi_\infty(E) if it existed, the equivalence is even easier in that case.

These first two definitions also imply the third, since the abelian cohomology H n(E,A)H^n(E,A) with coefficients in an abelian sheaf AA can be identified with nonabelian cohomology in the locally constant stack B nAB^n A which is the nn-fold delooping of AA, and locally constant stacks on EE are represented by maps out of Π ∞(E)\Pi_\infty(E).

Conversely, it is proven in Artin-Mazur that (in our language) a geometric morphism of 1-topoi satisfying the third definition necessarily induces isomorphisms on all homotopy progroups. (The idea is essentially that nonabelian cohomology is controlled by abelian cohomology with local coefficients, via Postnikov decompositions.) In the locally ∞\infty-connected case, this is equivalent to inducing an equivalence of fundamental ∞\infty-groupoids—the first definition. Thus, at least for locally ∞\infty-connected 1-topoi, the first three definitions are all equivalent.

Adjunctions induce homotopy equivalences

One important thing to note is that any adjunction in the 2-category Topos induces a homotopy equivalence in any of the above senses. For the first (hence also the second and third) sense, we just observe that Π ∞\Pi_\infty is 2-functorial, whereas it lands in the (∞,1)-category ∞Gpd where all 2-morphisms are invertible; hence adjunctions get sent to adjunctions, which are necessarily equivalences.

For the final definition, we note that has tensors with the interval category, that are given by cartesian product with sheaves on the Sierpinski space Σ\Sigma. Thus, a natural transformation between two geometric morphisms E→FE\to F is the same as a single geometric morphism E×Sh(Σ)→FE\times Sh(\Sigma)\to F. Now just pick a map [0,1]→Σ[0,1]\to \Sigma that doesn’t identify the endpoints, like the classifying map of [0,12][0,\frac1 2], to get an actual “homotopy” between the same two geometric morphisms. Hence, any 2-morphism in ToposTopos gives a homotopy, so any adjunction gives a homotopy equivalence.

In particular, this means that any local geometric morphism or totally connected geometric morphism is a homotopy equivalence.
