Idea

The concept of dominance is a weakening of the concept of surjectivity for geometric morphisms. It generalizes the concept of a continuous function whose image is a dense subspace of its codomain in topology.

Definition

A geometric morphism f:ℱ→ℰf:\mathcal{F}\to\mathcal{E} is called dominant if the following equivalent conditions hold:

  • The direct image satisfies: f *(∅ ℱ)≅∅ ℰf_\ast(\emptyset_\mathcal{F})\cong\emptyset_\mathcal{E}.

  • The inverse image satisfies: from f *(Z)≅∅ ℱf^\ast(Z)\cong\emptyset_\mathcal{F} follows Z≅∅ ℰZ\cong\emptyset_\mathcal{E}.

  • The inverse image satisfies: from f *(Z)≅∅ ℱf^\ast(Z)\cong\emptyset_\mathcal{F} follows Z≅∅ ℰZ\cong\emptyset_\mathcal{E} for ZZ a subterminal object.

Examples

  • A geometric morphism f:Sh(X)→Sh(Y)f:Sh(X)\to Sh(Y) between the toposes of sheaves on two topological spaces X,YX, Y is dominant iff the corresponding continuous map f:X→Yf:X\to Y has a dense image f(X)f(X) in YY.

  • A geometric embedding i:ℰ↪ℱi:\mathcal{E}\hookrightarrow\mathcal{F} is dominant precisely when it exhibits ℰ\mathcal{E} as a dense subtopos.

Properties

Proposition

If f:ℱ→ℰf:\mathcal{F}\to\mathcal{E} is a surjection (i.e. f *f^\ast is faithful) then ff is dominant.

Proof: Suppose f *(Z)≅∅ ℱf^\ast(Z)\cong \emptyset_\mathcal{F} is initial, then Hom ℱ(∅ ℱ,f *(Y))=Hom ℱ(f *(Z),f *(Y))Hom_\mathcal{F}(\emptyset_\mathcal{F},f^\ast(Y))=Hom_\mathcal{F}(f^\ast(Z),f^\ast(Y)) is a singleton for all Y∈ℰY\in\mathcal{E} , but by faithfulness of f *f^\ast this implies that Hom ℰ(Z,Y)Hom_\mathcal{E}(Z,Y) is a singleton for all Y∈ℰY\in\mathcal{E} , which says that ZZ is initial in ℰ\mathcal{E}. ▪\qed

Proposition

Let f:ℱ→ℰf:\mathcal{F}\to\mathcal{E} be a geometric morphism and i∘si\circ s its surjection-inclusion factorization. f:ℱ→ℰf:\mathcal{F}\to\mathcal{E} is dominant iff i:Im(f)↪ℰi:Im(f)\hookrightarrow\mathcal{E} is a dense inclusion.

Proof: Suppose ii is dense, hence dominant. ss as a surjection is dominant as well, and so is their composition ff.

Conversely, suppose f:ℱ→ℰf:\mathcal{F}\to\mathcal{E} is dominant and i *(Z)≅∅ Im(f)i^\ast(Z)\cong \emptyset_{Im(f)}. Since s *s^\ast preserves colimits, ∅ ℱ≅s *(∅ Im(f))≅s *∘i *(Z)=f *(Z)\emptyset_\mathcal{F}\cong s^\ast (\emptyset_{Im(f)})\cong s^\ast\circ i^\ast (Z)=f^\ast(Z) but ff is dominant by assumption, therefore Z≅∅ ℰZ\cong \emptyset_\mathcal{E}, hence ii is dense. ▪\qed

The following is a slight generalization of the (dense,closed)-factorization employing dominant geometric morphisms:

Proposition

Let f:ℱ→ℰf:\mathcal{F}\to\mathcal{E} be a geometric morphism. Then ff factors as a dominant geometric morphism dd followed by a closed inclusion cc.

Proof: Let i∘d 1i\circ d_1 be the surjection-inclusion factorization of ff. Since d 1d_1 is surjective, it is dominant (cf. above). Then we use the (dense,closed)-factorization to factor ii into c∘d 2c\circ d_2. Since both d id_i are dominant, so is d:=d 2∘d 1d:=d_2\circ d_1 and c∘dc\circ d yields the demanded factorization of ff. ▪\qed

Remark

The concept can be generalized to morphisms in a topos ℰ\mathcal{E} by calling f:X→Yf:X\to Y dominant if the induced geometric morphism f *⊣∏ f:ℰ/X→ℰ/Yf^\ast\dashv\prod_f:\mathcal{E}/X\to\mathcal{E}/Y is dominant, where f *f^\ast denotes the pullback functor.
