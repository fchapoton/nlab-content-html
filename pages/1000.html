Definition

A monad (T,μ,i)(T,\mu,i) on the category Set of sets, is finitary (also called algebraic, although some people consider any monad to be an algebraic notion) if the underlying endofunctor T:Set→SetT:\mathrm{Set}\to\mathrm{Set} commutes with filtered colimits.

In other words, an algebraic monad is a monoid in the category of algebraic endofunctors on Set\mathrm{Set}.

Properties

A finitary monad (T,μ,i)(T,\mu,i) is completely determined by its value on all finite ordinals n∈ℕ 0n\in\mathbb{N}_0 considered as standard finite sets. T(n)T(n) is then the set of nn-ary operations. The notion of algebraic monad is hence similar to the notion of a nonsymmetric operad in Set\mathrm{Set}, but it is not equivalent, because of the possibility of duplicating or discarding inputs.

More precisely, each finitary monad TT defines a Lawvere theory Th TTh_T, namely Th T=Free fin opTh_T = Free_{fin}^{op} where Free finFree_{fin} is the category of free algebras T(n)T(n) on finite sets (as a full subcategory of Alg TAlg_T). In fact, the two notions are equivalent: the assignment

T↦Th TT \mapsto Th_T

defines an equivalence between the category of finitary monads on SetSet and the category of Lawvere theories. Moreover, the category of TT-algebras is equivalent to the category of models of Th TTh_T. However, a technical advantage of Lawvere theories is that they can be interpreted in categories other than Set: a model of a Lawvere theory 𝒯\mathcal{T} in a category with cartesian products CC is just a product-preserving functor 𝒯→C\mathcal{T} \to C.

Applications

There is an interesting commutativity condition singling out the subclass of commutative algebraic/finitary monads, cf. commutative algebraic theory; they are useful to establish a theory of generalized commutative schemes.
